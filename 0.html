Introduction

A solution is a homogeneous mixture of two or more components. The component in the largest quantity is the solvent; others are solutes.

Expression of Concentration
Mass Percentage

Mass % of a component = A@ <span style="color:blue;">(Mass of component / Total mass of solution) × 100</span>

Volume Percentage

Volume % = A@ <span style="color:blue;">(Volume of solute / Volume of solution) × 100</span>

Mole Fraction

Mole fraction (x) = A@ <span style="color:blue;">n₁ / (n₁ + n₂)</span>

Molarity (M)

M = A@ <span style="color:blue;">Moles of solute / Volume of solution in L</span>

Molality (m)

m = A@ <span style="color:blue;">Moles of solute / Mass of solvent in kg</span>

IMPORTANT: Molarity depends on temperature, but molality does not.
Solubility
Solids in Liquids

Solubility generally increases with temperature.

Gases in Liquids

Solubility decreases with increase in temperature.

REMEMBER: Solubility of gases is governed by Henry’s Law.
Henry’s Law

"Solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the pressure of gas above the liquid."

Formula: A@ <span style="color:blue;">p = KH × x</span>

Where:

p = Partial pressure of gas

KH = Henry’s law constant

x = Mole fraction of gas

Raoult’s Law

"The partial vapour pressure of each component in a solution is directly proportional to its mole fraction."

For A: A@ <span style="color:blue;">PA = P°A × xA</span>

For B: A@ <span style="color:blue;">PB = P°B × xB</span>

Total: A@ <span style="color:blue;">Ptotal = PA + PB</span>

IMPORTANT: Raoult's law is applicable only for ideal solutions.
Ideal and Non-Ideal Solutions
Ideal Solutions

- Obey Raoult’s law at all compositions

- A@ <span style="color:blue;">ΔHmix = 0</span>, A@ <span style="color:blue;">ΔVmix = 0</span>

- Example: Benzene + Toluene

Non-Ideal Solutions

- Deviate from Raoult’s law

- A@ <span style="color:blue;">ΔHmix ≠ 0</span>, A@ <span style="color:blue;">ΔVmix ≠ 0</span>

IMPORTANT: Positive deviation → A–B < A–A or B–B (e.g., ethanol + acetone)
IMPORTANT: Negative deviation → A–B > A–A or B–B (e.g., HCl + water)
Azeotropes

Abnormal Molar Mass

When a solute dissociates or associates, molar mass changes from the expected value.

Van’t Hoff Factor (i)

A@ <span style="color:blue;">i = Normal molar mass / Abnormal molar mass = Observed colligative property / Calculated colligative property</span>

For Dissociation

i > 1

For Association

i < 1

REMEMBER: NaCl → i ≈ 2 (dissociates)
REMEMBER: Glucose → i ≈ 1 (no dissociation)
